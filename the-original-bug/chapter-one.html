The Original Bug

Chapter One

We Built It In Our Image

Sandy B. Patterson

Section 1.1

The Brief

On May 25, 2023, a lawyer named Steven Schwartz filed a brief in a United States federal court. The brief cited several cases in support of his client’s position, as briefs do. It named the parties, summarized the holdings, and presented them as legal precedents that bore on the matter at hand. There was one problem. Several of the cases did not exist.

Schwartz, a partner at the New York firm Levidow, Levidow & Oberman, had asked ChatGPT for help researching the case. ChatGPT had provided cases. Schwartz had not verified them. He did not know, apparently, that large language models could produce confident and specific citations to cases that had never been decided, parties that had never appeared before any court, holdings that no judge had ever written. He had treated the output of an AI system the way he might treat a research assistant with a law degree and twenty years of experience: as essentially trustworthy.

The judge was not sympathetic. Judge P. Kevin Castel of the Southern District of New York ordered Schwartz and his firm to pay a fine of five thousand dollars and issued a written opinion that described the situation with an economy of language that courtroom prose rarely achieves: the brief contained “bogus judicial decisions with bogus quotes and bogus internal citations.” The case attracted international attention, became a landmark example in the ongoing debate about AI in professional practice, and earned Schwartz a permanent place in the footnotes of a technology story that is still being written.

What interested me about the case was not the mistake. Lawyers have made mistakes, including spectacular ones, since the invention of law. What interested me was the cognitive sequence that produced it. Schwartz asked the machine a question. The machine answered confidently. He moved on. At no point — or not soon enough — did he perform the verification step that lawyers are trained to perform, that law schools insist upon, that the entire practice of legal research is built around: check the citation. He skipped it. Not out of laziness, most likely, but out of a much more interesting failure: he extended to the machine the same default trust he would extend to a competent human colleague.

That extension is not irrational. It is, in fact, the most natural thing in the world. We have been calibrated by evolution and experience to treat fluent, confident, contextually appropriate language as a signal of knowledge. When a person speaks to us in complete sentences, with specific details, in a register that matches the domain, we take this as evidence that they know what they are talking about. We do not do this foolishly — we do it because, across tens of thousands of years of human interaction, it has mostly been correct. Fluent confidence is a reasonable proxy for competence. It is just not a reliable proxy for AI.

This is the first thing this book wants you to understand, and it is worth sitting with before we go any further: the problem is not that the AI lied. The problem is that it did not know it was wrong. It produced the citations the way it produces everything — by predicting, token by token, what plausible legal language looks like. It had no access to a database of real decisions. It had no way to check its own output. It had no concept of “this case exists” versus “this case sounds like it should exist.” It was, in the most precise technical sense, doing exactly what it was designed to do: generating text that fit the pattern of a competent legal brief.

Steven Schwartz was not deceived by a machine that was trying to deceive him. He was deceived by a machine that was doing its job, and by his own cognitive system, which was doing what cognitive systems do: extend trust where the surface signals seem to warrant it, and skip the verification step when it feels like it ought to be unnecessary.

The original bug, in other words, was not in the software. It was in the user. It was in us.

The question of why we extend this trust — and why we find it so difficult to withdraw it even when we have been burned — is the subject of this chapter. To answer it properly, we need to go back considerably further than 2023. We need to go back to the basic architecture of the human mind: the systems we use to evaluate information, the shortcuts we use to manage the overwhelming complexity of the world, and the ancient and persistent preference for pattern over uncertainty that makes us both extraordinary reasoners and predictably exploitable ones. We need, in short, to understand cognition before we can understand what the AI is doing to it.
